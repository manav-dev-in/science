# 1. The Invisible World

Look around you.

You can see:

* Trees
* Birds
* Dogs
* Humans
* Ants

But can you see bacteria?

No.

Can you see Amoeba?

No.

Can you see yeast?

No.

These organisms are **too small** for our eyes.

Our eyes can only see objects above a certain size. Smaller objects require special instruments. 

---

## Why can't we see them?

The human eye has a limit.

Anything much smaller than about **0.1 mm (100 μm)** becomes difficult or impossible to see clearly.

For example

| Organism      | Visible? |
| ------------- | -------- |
| Human         | ✅        |
| Dog           | ✅        |
| Ant           | ✅        |
| Grain of sand | ✅        |
| Hair          | ✅        |
| Onion cell    | ❌        |
| Bacteria      | ❌        |
| Virus         | ❌        |

---

## So how did scientists discover them?

They invented tools that **magnify** objects.

First came:

* lenses

Then:

* magnifying glasses

Finally:

* microscopes

These instruments make tiny objects appear much larger. 

---

# 2. Lens

A **lens** is a transparent curved piece of glass (or plastic).

It bends light.

This makes objects appear larger or smaller depending on the type of lens.

The textbook mentions that early lenses looked like lentil seeds—thicker in the middle and thinner at the edges—which is where the word "lens" comes from. 

---

## Magnifying Glass

A magnifying glass is simply a convex lens.

It makes nearby objects appear larger.

Example:

Instead of seeing an ant like this:

•

You see:

🐜

with much greater detail.

---

# Activity (Very Important)

The textbook asks you to fill a round-bottom flask with water and look through it.

The water-filled flask behaves like a magnifying lens.

Letters appear larger because light bends when passing through the curved water surface. 

---

### Exam Question

Why do letters appear bigger through a flask filled with water?

Answer:

Because the flask filled with water acts like a magnifying lens.

# 3. Microscope

A microscope is an instrument used to observe very small objects that cannot be seen with the naked eye.

It magnifies objects many times.

Without microscopes we would never have discovered:

* cells
* bacteria
* protozoa
* blood cells
* microorganisms

---

## Types

For Grade 8, just know:

### Simple microscope

One lens

Less magnification

---

### Compound microscope

Multiple lenses

Higher magnification

Used in laboratories

---

# Fun Fact

Modern microscopes can magnify objects thousands—even millions—of times (electron microscopes), although your textbook mainly discusses simple microscopes and early compound microscopes. This is extra knowledge.

This is probably the MOST IMPORTANT definition in the chapter.

## Definition

A **cell** is the **basic structural and functional unit of life**.

Every living organism is made of one or more cells.

No house.

No living organism.

---

This experiment appears in many exams.

# 9. Main Parts of a Cell

Every basic cell has three essential parts:

1. Cell membrane
2. Cytoplasm
3. Nucleus

Plant cells also have a **cell wall** outside the membrane. 

---

# A. Cell Membrane

Also called:

Plasma membrane

### Function

* surrounds the cell
* protects it
* separates one cell from another
* allows useful materials to enter
* allows waste to leave

Think of it as the security gate of a building.

---

# B. Cytoplasm

The jelly-like material inside the cell.

### Function

Most life processes happen here.

It contains:

* proteins
* carbohydrates
* fats
* mineral salts
* other cell components

Think of it as the factory floor where the work happens.

---

# C. Nucleus

Often called:

The brain of the cell.

### Functions

* controls cell activities
* regulates growth

Without the nucleus, the cell cannot properly coordinate its functions.

---

# D. Cell Wall (Plant Cells Only)

Found only in plant cells.

### Functions

* gives strength
* provides support
* maintains shape
* protects the cell

Animal cells do **not** have a cell wall.

---

# Quick Memory Trick

**M C N**

**Membrane**
→ Controls entry and exit

**Cytoplasm**
→ Carries out life processes

**Nucleus**
→ Controls the cell

---

# Plant Cell vs Animal Cell

| Plant Cell            | Animal Cell               |
| --------------------- | ------------------------- |
| Cell wall present     | Cell wall absent          |
| Chloroplasts present  | Chloroplasts absent       |
| Large central vacuole | Small or no vacuoles      |
| Usually rectangular   | Usually irregular/rounded |
| More rigid            | More flexible             |

The textbook also notes that chloroplasts help in photosynthesis and that plant cells usually have a large vacuole for storage and support. 

---

# ⭐ Exam Tip

Don't memorize this chapter word-for-word.

Instead, understand **why** each part exists:

* The membrane regulates what enters and leaves.
* The cytoplasm is where most activities happen.
* The nucleus controls the cell.
* The cell wall gives plant cells strength.
* Chloroplasts enable photosynthesis.

If you understand the purpose, you'll remember the facts much more easily.

# 10. Different Types of Cells

One question many students ask is:

> **If every organism is made of cells, why don't all cells look the same?**

Excellent question.

Different cells have **different jobs**.

Just like workers in a company:

* Manager
* Engineer
* Security Guard
* Accountant

All work in the same company but have different duties.

Similarly,

Different cells perform different functions.

So they have different:

* Shapes
* Sizes
* Structures

The textbook emphasizes that a cell's shape and structure are related to its function. 

---

# Example 1 — Muscle Cell

Shape:

* Long
* Spindle-shaped

Function:

* Contracts and relaxes

This allows:

* Walking
* Running
* Blinking
* Heart beating

### Memory Trick

Muscles need to stretch.

Therefore,

Muscle cells are long.

---

# Example 2 — Nerve Cell (Neuron)

Probably the coolest cell in your body.

Shape:

Very long

With many branches.

Function:

Carries messages.

Example:

You touch a hot pan.

Within milliseconds,

The nerve cells carry a message:

Finger → Brain

Brain → Hand

Move away!

The long shape helps messages travel quickly across the body. 

---

# Example 3 — Cheek Cell

Shape:

Thin

Flat

Polygonal

Function:

Forms a protective covering inside the mouth.

---

# Example 4 — Plant Cells

Plant cells also vary.

Some are:

* Rectangular
* Tube-like
* Long
* Oval

Tube-like cells transport water throughout the plant. 

---

# Golden Rule

> **Structure follows function.**

Whenever you see a weird-shaped cell,

Ask yourself:

"What job does it do?"

The answer usually explains the shape.

# 2. Magnetic Effect of Electric Current

## Definition

The **magnetic effect of electric current** is the phenomenon in which a conductor carrying electric current produces a magnetic field around it. 

---

# Important Rule

No Current

↓

No Magnetic Field

Current Flows

↓

Magnetic Field Appears

Current Stops

# 3. Magnetic Field

Imagine placing an invisible bubble around a wire.

Inside this invisible region,

Magnetic force exists.

This region is called the **magnetic field**.

### Definition

A magnetic field is the region around a magnet or a current-carrying conductor where its magnetic effect can be felt. 

---

# How do we know a magnetic field exists?

We cannot see it.

Instead,

We observe its effect.

Example:

A compass needle moves.

That proves a magnetic field is present.

Think of wind:

You can't see wind,

But you can see leaves moving.

Similarly,

You can't see a magnetic field,

But you can see the compass needle move.

# 4. Compass

A compass is actually

a tiny magnet.

It always points roughly:

North ↔ South

because Earth itself behaves like a giant magnet.

When another magnetic field appears nearby,

the needle changes direction.

That is called

**Deflection**. 

---

# Exam Definition

### Deflection

The movement of a compass needle away from its original north-south direction due to a magnetic field.

---

# 6. Electromagnet

One of the most important topics.

Question:

YES.

---

# Definition

An **electromagnet** is a magnet produced when electric current flows through a coil of wire, usually wound around an iron core. 

---

# Construction

You need:

✔ Copper wire

✔ Battery

---

# Why does this happen?

Current flowing through the coil creates a magnetic field.

The iron nail becomes magnetized.

Once the current stops,

the magnetic field disappears,

and the nail is no longer a magnet.

This is why it's called a **temporary magnet**. 

---

# Permanent Magnet vs Electromagnet

| Permanent Magnet       | Electromagnet                    |
| ---------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Always magnetic        | Magnetic only when current flows |
| Cannot be switched off | Can be switched on/off           |
| Fixed strength         | Strength can be changed          |
| Examples: bar magnet   | Example: iron nail with wire     |

Iron is a magnetic material.

# Heating Effect of Electric Current

## Definition

The **heating effect of electric current** is the production of heat when electric current passes through a conductor. 

---

# Why does a wire become hot?

Imagine water flowing through a pipe.

If the pipe is narrow,

Water faces difficulty moving.

Similarly,

Electric current also faces opposition while flowing through a wire.

This opposition is called

## Resistance

---

# Resistance

### Definition

**Resistance is the opposition offered by a conductor to the flow of electric current.**

The higher the resistance,

The more heat is produced.

The textbook explains that different conductors offer different levels of resistance and that this resistance converts some electrical energy into heat.

↓

Less heat

↓

↓

More heat

# 🚨 Common Exam Mistakes

❌ **"An electromagnet is always magnetic."**
✅ It is magnetic **only while current flows**.

❌ **"Nichrome is used because it is a good conductor."**
✅ It is used because it has **high resistance** and can withstand high temperatures.

❌ **"All batteries can be recharged."**
✅ Only **rechargeable batteries** can be recharged.

❌ **"Heat is produced only in nichrome wires."**
✅ **Every conductor** produces some heat when current flows. Nichrome simply produces **more** because of its higher resistance.

❌ **"Current creates a permanent magnetic field."**
✅ The magnetic field exists **only while current is flowing**.
